[Campus](https://paarth-r.github.io/Campus) started as a tech demo: point a webcam at
yourself, reach for glowing holds, and a ragdoll climbs a scrolling wall. It was fun
for about thirty seconds. The physics were floppy in the wrong way, the climber
stretched its arms to grab holds it had no business reaching, and there was nothing to
*do*. This is the log of the session that turned it into something I'd actually call a
game — still a single `index.html`, no build step, [live on GitHub
Pages](https://paarth-r.github.io/Campus).

## The shape of it

The climber is a **Verlet ragdoll**. You play in one of two modes: **Campus** uses
webcam pose (hands only, feet dangle — a hand grabs when its on-screen marker overlaps
a hold), and **Klifur** is a pure mouse puzzle where you drag each limb onto holds.
Falls are caught by the rope at your last clipped quickdraw. Everything below is about
making that core mechanic feel like climbing instead of like dragging a corpse up a
wall.

## Bending limbs

The first version drew each arm and leg as a single stretched segment. That's why the
climber could "reach" a hold across the screen — the arm just got longer. The fix was
to make limbs **two-bone**: shoulder → elbow → hand, pelvis → knee → foot. Now elbows
fold and knees bow, and crucially the maximum hand distance is the actual arm length.
An out-of-reach hold simply *can't* be grabbed; the arm stays at full extension instead
of snapping out to it.

This also gave the Reach stat something physical to do — upgrading Reach literally
lengthens the arms, which is how you unlock bigger moves.

## Precision grab

The old grab test used the raw cursor position, so you could "grab" a hold from a
distance as long as the cursor was near it. The new rule is overlap-only: dropping a
hand grabs from where the hand *actually* is (clamped to reach), and only if the hand's
circle overlaps the hold's. No more telekinesis.

## Leg drive that doesn't launch you

Legs were the hardest thing to get right. The first attempt had the climber always
auto-standing, which looked stiff and fought the ragdoll. I replaced it with something
input-gated and directional:

- **No input** → the ragdoll hangs loose, limp and gravity-sagged.
- **While dragging** → planted feet push the pelvis *toward* the drag direction. You
  use your legs to push the same way you use your arms to pull.

The trick to keeping it stable was to push toward a target *below* the rigid
leg-length limit — so the legs provide force without ever fighting the hard
constraint. Push too hard against a hard constraint and the solver launches the
climber off the wall. Push softly toward a slack target and it just... climbs.

## Stamina

A BotW-style green stamina ring sits by the active hand. The rule took two passes to
get right: **stamina drains only while you're actively moving a hand to a new hold** —
the effort of *moving*, not of hanging. Both hands on, both off, or a still one-handed
hang all refresh it; only a committed reach drains it. Run it to zero mid-move and your
grip blows. Grip and Endurance stats raise the pool.

## Making it a game

Physics alone is a toy. The progression loop is what makes it a game, and it's all
localStorage-backed:

- A **climber profile** that persists points, four skills (Grip · Endurance · Fingers ·
  Reach), sends, and best times.
- **Skills feed the mechanic**, not just a number: Reach lengthens arms, Grip/Endurance
  grow the stamina pool, and **Fingers** gate small crimps — micro-holds slip until your
  fingers are strong enough.
- **V-grades with a soft gate.** Routes up to your skill+1 are unlocked; harder ones
  show a lock and the grade you need. The built-in ladder is authored V0 → V5; custom
  levels are auto-graded by a difficulty heuristic. Route geometry is generated against
  a *fixed* reference reach, so leveling up never mutates the walls themselves.
- An **economy** — first ascents pay points by grade, later PBs pay a little, and you
  spend points in a Training screen to level skills and unlock harder routes.

The grades cap at **V17** (real climbing's hardest grade). The auto-grader can propose
V18 on an absurd custom wall, but it's capped with a "(you proposed V18 — capped)"
note, and anything above V10 gets a 🤨 and a *"really bro?"* jab on the chip.

## The restyle, and two bugs hiding in the CSS

I threw out the old cream/sage theme for an **alpine field-guide / topographic** look:
warm sandstone paper, lichen-sage, rust-clay accent, contour-line textures, and
[Fraunces](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Fraunces) paired with Hanken Grotesk.
Earthy and distinctive, but calm enough to read over a live canvas.

Rewriting the CSS surfaced two structural bugs that had nothing to do with the game
logic:

1. The **editor toolbar used `flex-wrap`**, so a long instructional tip wrapped it onto
   multiple rows — and the side panels, pinned with a hardcoded `top`, slid underneath
   it. Fix: a fixed-height, no-wrap toolbar row with the side panels pinned off
   `calc(barHeight + gutter)`.
2. The **HUD had no `.hud.hidden { display: none }` rule**, so the in-game back button
   and hint leaked into the editor and draw screens.

Neither was a physics bug. Both were the kind of thing that only shows up once you
care about the thing looking finished.

## What's next

The climb is mechanically sound now but path-dependent — good technique actually
matters, which is the point. Open threads: tuning the leg-push and stamina-drain feel,
and some proportion polish on the climber. But it's a real game now.
